Why AI-Savvy Clinician?

AI-Ready Clinician gave you the confidence to engage. AI-Steady Clinician gave you the knowledge to evaluate. AI-Savvy Clinician gives you the capability to create.

There is a proliferation of guidelines, checklists, and frameworks for AI tool development — so many that developers and stakeholders often feel overwhelmed and overlook the most essential practices. ETEPAI solves this by integrating 30 frameworks into one compact, comprehensive guide structured across 4 stages and 3 domains. This programme teaches you to apply it.

By the end of this programme, you will be able to design or co-design an AI project with the right team composition, the right ethical safeguards, the right technical standards, and the right epidemiological rigour — with confidence.

The ETEPAI framework in one sentence
Comprehensive, complete, compact, and crisp consideration from conception to execution — promoting high-quality, ethically sound, and clinically relevant AI tools across four stages (Design → Develop → Deploy → Post-deployment) and three domains (Ethics · Technical · Epidemiological).

Session 1 · 75 min · Orientation

The ETEPAI framework & readiness probes

Understand why ETEPAI was created, how it differs from existing frameworks, and how to use the probing questions to assess whether your project is ready to proceed at each stage.

Framework orientation Readiness assessment

Why ETEPAI? The proliferation problem

There are now over 30 major guidelines, checklists, and frameworks for AI tool development in healthcare. Each is valuable. But overwhelmed by many similar yet different and lengthy referent materials, developers and stakeholders may overlook the most relevant and essential practices — rendering good materials less effective and stalling improvement in AI tool production.

Common consequences: lack of data and code availability, absence of or small human comparator groups, shortage of real-world clinical relevance, transparency failures, and inappropriate conclusions — causing high risk of bias and research waste. ETEPAI was created to integrate 30 frameworks into one compact yet comprehensive guide.

What makes ETEPAI different
ETEPAI is not another checklist to replace the others. It is an annotated content page — like a book's table of contents — that signposts you to the necessity of each step and directs you to the right referenced materials for deeper reading. It is comprehensive enough for trained personnel and clear enough as a guide for unprepared stakeholders.

The three domains explained

Ethics domain
Addresses the moral dimensions of AI tool development: patient safety, fairness, transparency, accountability, data rights, and the societal impacts of algorithmic decision-making. Aligns with EU trustworthiness standards, WHO AI ethics principles, and local regulatory requirements (e.g. MMC guideline). Every stage has ethical obligations that cannot be deferred to post-deployment.
Technical domain
Covers the engineering and data science requirements: problem framing, data curation, model building, infrastructure, validation pipelines, deployment architecture, and post-deployment monitoring. The clinician does not need to execute these — but must know enough to ask the right questions and detect when corners are being cut.
Epidemiological domain
Applies the rigour of clinical research methods to AI tool development: study design, outcome definition, population selection, sample size, bias control, statistical analysis, and real-world evidence generation. This is the domain where clinicians have the most unique expertise — and where AI projects most frequently fall short.

Probing questions — are you ready to proceed?

These questions are asked before entering each stage. If you cannot answer them, the project is not yet ready to proceed. Tap each stage to expand.

Stage 1 Design — readiness probes
▼
Is this a real clinical problem that AI can genuinely help solve — or is this an AI solution looking for a problem?
Have you defined the clinical problem in terms of a measurable outcome, a target population, and a specific clinical setting?
Have you identified all relevant stakeholders — clinicians, patients, data custodians, ethicists, administrators — and planned for their involvement?
Do you have access to sufficient, representative, and high-quality data, or a realistic plan to obtain it?
Have you obtained or planned for ethical approval, data governance agreements, and PDPA compliance?
Is the intended use diagnostic, predictive, prescriptive, or administrative — and does your study design match this intent?
What happens if the AI tool fails, gives wrong outputs, or is misused? Have you mapped out the risk scenarios?
Stage 2 Development — readiness probes
▼
Are your data labels clinically meaningful and consistently applied? What is the inter-rater reliability of your annotators?
Is your training dataset representative of the population your model will be deployed on — in terms of age, sex, ethnicity, and disease severity?
Have you planned for information leakage prevention — particularly temporal leakage and train-test contamination?
Which performance metrics will you use, and are they appropriate for the clinical task? (Sensitivity/specificity for screening, AUC for discrimination, calibration for probability estimation?)
How will you assess fairness — specifically, will the model perform equitably across demographic subgroups relevant to your patient population?
Is your code version-controlled, documented, and reproducible? Can another team replicate your results?
Stage 3 Deployment — readiness probes
▼
Has the model been externally validated on data from a different institution, time period, or population to the training set?
Has sandboxing been completed — has the tool run in a real clinical environment without affecting care decisions, allowing performance assessment?
Is the tool registered with the relevant regulatory authority if it meets the definition of a medical device?
Is there a clinician override mechanism built in — can clinical staff accept, reject, or modify the tool's recommendation without friction?
Has the alert interface been designed to minimise alert fatigue — are thresholds, timing, and presentation optimised with clinical staff input?
Are staff trained? Is there a clearly communicated escalation pathway for unexpected or concerning outputs?
Stage 4 Post-deployment — readiness probes
▼
What performance metrics are being monitored continuously — and what threshold triggers a model review or withdrawal?
Is there a mechanism for clinical staff to report unexpected model behaviour, near misses, or patient harm?
How will you detect model drift — the gradual degradation of performance as patient populations, clinical practices, or data collection methods change over time?
Is a prospective real-world evidence study or RCT planned to evaluate actual clinical impact — not just model performance metrics?
Is there a health economic evaluation planned — does the benefit justify the cost of continued deployment?
Who owns the ongoing governance of this tool — and is that accountability clear, resourced, and exercised?

Session 2 · 75 min · Research design

Design stage — ethics, technical & epidemiological

The design stage aligns AI tool development with epidemiological research methodology. Getting this right determines everything that follows. This is where clinicians contribute most uniquely.

Stage 1: Design Ethics · Technical · Epi

Ethics domain — design stage

  • Beneficence and non-maleficence — articulate clearly how this tool benefits patients and what harms it could cause. Map risk scenarios before any development begins.
  • Fairness by design — identify which patient subgroups might be disadvantaged. Plan data collection and validation to actively test for disparate performance across sex, age, ethnicity, and socioeconomic status.
  • Transparency and explainability — decide now whether the clinical context requires an explainable model (high-stakes decisions) or whether a black-box model is acceptable (lower-stakes administrative tasks).
  • Data rights and consent — is the use of this data covered by existing consents? Does the PDPA framework require new consent? What are the data sharing agreement requirements?
  • Stakeholder engagement — have patients been involved in defining the problem? Have frontline staff who will use the tool been consulted on its design?
  • Conflict of interest — are there commercial interests that could bias tool development? Are funding sources declared and governance structures independent?

Technical domain — design stage

  • Problem framing — is this a classification, regression, clustering, or generation task? The algorithm family follows from the problem type, not from what is fashionable.
  • Algorithm selection rationale — document why this algorithm family was chosen over alternatives. Simpler models that perform well are usually preferable to complex models that are marginally better but opaque.
  • Data availability audit — catalogue what data exists, in what format, with what quality issues, under what governance, and in whose custody.
  • Infrastructure planning — cloud vs on-premise vs federated? Who maintains the infrastructure? What are the cost and security implications?
  • Team composition — has the full required team been identified, including clinical champion, data scientist, ML engineer, data governance officer, ethicist, and patient representative?

Epidemiological domain — design stage

The design stage aligns with epidemiological research methodology
This is the domain where clinicians are most expert — and most needed. Every AI tool is ultimately a clinical study. It has a research question, a study design, a target population, an exposure (the AI input), an outcome (the prediction target), and sources of bias. Applying epidemiological rigour at the design stage prevents the most common and most serious failures.
  • PICO framework — Population (who?), Intervention (what does the AI do?), Comparator (compared to what — clinician decision alone? existing tool?), Outcome (what measurable change in care or outcomes?)
  • Study design — diagnostic accuracy study? Prediction model study (TRIPOD-AI)? Clinical trial (CONSORT-AI)? The design determines the reporting framework required.
  • Outcome definition — what is the ground truth? How is it measured, by whom, with what inter-rater agreement? Poorly defined outcomes produce unreliable models.
  • Sample size — how many cases are needed for adequate model training and separate validation? Rule of thumb: at least 10 events per variable for prediction models — often requiring thousands of clinical events.
  • Selection bias — does your dataset systematically exclude certain patients? Missing data is rarely random in clinical settings.

Session 3 · 75 min · Technical execution

Development stage — building with rigour

The development stage emphasises transparent project execution. This is where technical decisions are made and data science happens — but clinicians have critical responsibilities in labelling, validation oversight, and bias detection.

Stage 2: Develop Ethics · Technical · Epi

Ethics domain — development stage

  • Bias detection protocols — actively test model performance across subgroups (age, sex, ethnicity, comorbidities) throughout training. Do not wait until validation to discover disparate performance.
  • Audit trails — every decision made during development — data exclusions, label revisions, model iterations — must be documented. This is accountability in practice, not just principle.
  • Transparency commitments — are model weights, training code, and data processing scripts being version-controlled and prepared for sharing upon publication?
  • Intellectual property and data sharing — who owns the trained model? What obligations exist to share data or code? Are these agreed before the model is built, not after?

Technical domain — development stage

  • Data preprocessing standards — document every preprocessing decision: imputation strategy, normalisation method, outlier handling, class imbalance correction. These decisions significantly affect model performance and must be reproducible.
  • Train/validation/test split — never allow data from the test set to influence preprocessing, feature selection, or hyperparameter tuning. Information leakage is the single most common source of inflated reported performance.
  • Model selection rationale — document why the final model was chosen over alternatives. Include performance on the validation set, not just the training set.
  • Internal validation — cross-validation on the training set. Necessary but not sufficient. Does not replace external validation on independent data.
  • Code documentation and version control — use Git. Write readable code. Document dependencies. This is not optional — it is a scientific reproducibility requirement.

Epidemiological domain — development stage

  • Labelling strategy and inter-rater reliability — how were labels assigned? By one clinician or multiple? What is the kappa coefficient? Disagreements must be resolved by a defined protocol, not arbitrarily.
  • Class imbalance — in most clinical datasets, the outcome of interest (disease, deterioration, admission) is rare. Naive models predict "no event" for everyone and achieve 95% accuracy. Use appropriate methods: SMOTE, class weighting, threshold adjustment, and AUC rather than accuracy.
  • Confounding variable management — in clinical data, confounding is pervasive. A model that predicts mortality primarily because sick patients get more tests is learning proxy markers, not pathophysiology.
  • Performance metric selection — choose metrics before seeing results. AUC for discrimination. Calibration plots for probability estimation. Sensitivity/specificity at the clinically relevant operating threshold. All three, not just AUC.

This is a core ETEPAI development stage requirement.

Session 4 · 75 min · Real-world integration

Deploy & post-deployment — from model to clinical reality

Deployment and post-deployment stages focus on real-world implementation. A model that performs excellently in validation can still fail catastrophically in deployment. This is where governance and clinical ownership matter most.

Stages 3 & 4 Real-world integration

Ethics domain — deployment

  • Informed consent for AI-assisted decisions — do patients know an AI tool is being used in their care? Does your institution have a policy on disclosure? The MMC 2025 guideline requires clinicians to communicate AI benefits, risks, and limitations to facilitate informed consent.
  • Equitable access — does the deployment design ensure the tool benefits all patient groups equally, or will it disproportionately serve patients who are already well-resourced?
  • Clinician override mechanisms — a mandatory override mechanism is an ethical requirement, not a technical nicety. Clinicians must always be able to reject or modify AI recommendations without friction or documentation burden.
  • Alert design ethics — alert fatigue causes harm. Designing an AI that fires excessively is an ethical failure, not just a usability problem.

Technical domain — deployment

  • Sandboxing first — run the model in shadow mode for a defined period before it influences clinical decisions. Monitor performance on real patients without acting on outputs.
  • Integration architecture — how does the model receive input data, and how are outputs communicated to clinical staff? Who is responsible for the interface? What happens when the EMR system is updated?
  • Rollback procedures — what is the plan if the model needs to be withdrawn urgently? Who makes that decision, and how quickly can it happen?
  • Staff training — clinical staff must understand what the tool does, what it does not do, how to interpret its outputs, and how to report concerns. This is not optional.

Epidemiological domain — deployment & post-deployment

  • External validation in deployment setting — validate the model prospectively on the actual patient population in the actual clinical environment before full deployment.
  • Decision curve analysis — does acting on the model's recommendations actually improve clinical decisions across a range of threshold probabilities? Net benefit analysis quantifies clinical utility.
  • Health technology assessment — does the benefit justify the cost? What is the incremental cost-effectiveness ratio? This is increasingly required for institutional adoption decisions.
  • Prospective real-world evidence study — measuring model performance metrics is not the same as measuring clinical impact. A well-designed prospective study or RCT is the gold standard for demonstrating that the AI tool actually improves patient outcomes.
  • Post-market surveillance (model drift) — patient populations change, clinical practices evolve, and data collection methods shift. A model that performs well at deployment may degrade silently. Continuous monitoring with pre-specified drift detection thresholds is essential.
The accountability question every clinician must be able to answer
If this AI tool contributes to a patient harm — who is responsible? The clinician who acted on it? The hospital that deployed it without adequate governance? The vendor who built it without adequate validation? ETEPAI requires that accountability structures be defined before deployment — not assigned after harm.

No formal monitoring system is in place. According to ETEPAI post-deployment requirements, what is this an example of and what should have been in place?

The absence of this system is a governance failure, not a technical inevitability.
Look again
The scenario describes model drift — a real-world performance degradation over time as conditions change. This is expected in any deployed AI system, which is why ETEPAI's post-deployment requirements include continuous monitoring and pre-specified drift thresholds. Pitfalls & best practices Team builder Capstone

Common pitfalls — and how to prevent them

1
Solving the wrong problem
AI solution looking for a problem. The tool is technically impressive but clinically irrelevant or already addressed by simpler means.
Prevention: PICO framework at Design stage. Stakeholder consultation including frontline clinical staff and patients before any technical work begins.
2
Information leakage
Test set data contaminating training or preprocessing. Produces AUC values that are unrealistically high and cannot be reproduced in real-world use.
Prevention: strict data partitioning before any preprocessing; temporal splitting for time-series data; independent test set held by a third party until model is finalised.
3
Inadequate or biased training data
Data that excludes certain patient groups, reflects historical clinical biases, or is collected from a different population than the intended deployment setting.
Prevention: data audit for representativeness at Design stage; subgroup performance analysis during Development; external validation in the target population before Deployment.
4
No human comparator
Model performance reported without comparison to clinical decision-making. AUC of 0.85 vs what? An experienced clinician? Existing risk scores? Current standard of care?
Prevention: define the comparator at Design stage and include it in the study design. TRIPOD-AI requires human comparator reporting.
5
Internal validation only
Publication of models validated only on the training institution's data. Cannot be generalised. Frequently cited as "ready for clinical use" prematurely.
Prevention: plan external validation at Design stage as a mandatory step before any claim of generalisability or clinical utility.
6
No real-world evidence of clinical impact
Model accuracy does not equal patient benefit. A model that correctly predicts readmission risk does not improve outcomes unless clinical staff act on it effectively.
Prevention: plan a prospective real-world evidence study or RCT at Design stage. ETEPAI post-deployment stage requires measuring actual clinical impact.
7
Absent governance after deployment
The AI tool is deployed and forgotten. No monitoring, no drift detection, no incident pathway, no accountability structure. The tool degrades silently and causes harm.
Prevention: governance structure, monitoring plan, and accountability assignment are ETEPAI post-deployment requirements — agreed before deployment, not after.

Practical tool

AI Research Proposal Template

Based on the AI Research Proposal Outline (Multimedia Appendix 3, ETEPAI paper — Chew & Ngiam, JMIR AI 2026). Sections A–O follow the standard research proposal structure; the two final sections cover AI-specific disclosure and best practices. Tap each section to expand the guidance.

A.Topic and Abstract▼
A concise, informative title that captures the AI tool, the clinical problem, and the study design. The abstract should follow structured format: Background (why this matters), Objective (what the AI tool will do), Methods (study design and data approach), Expected Results (outcomes to be measured), and Conclusions (anticipated contribution). Keep to 250–300 words. Use AI tools to refine language but ensure clinical accuracy is your own.
B.Literature Review / Introduction▼
Summarise the current state of evidence on the clinical problem and existing AI solutions. Identify the gap your project fills. What has been tried? What has failed and why? What is missing from current tools — local population data, external validation, real-world impact evidence? Use tools such as Elicit, SciSpace, or Perplexity to assist literature searches, but critically appraise every source. End with a clear statement of the rationale for this specific AI project.
C.Theoretical / Conceptual Framework▼
Identify the theoretical underpinnings that inform the tool's purpose. Which clinical or behavioural theory explains the problem being addressed? What assumptions are you making about how clinicians, patients, or systems will interact with the AI output? For example: if predicting medication non-adherence, what adherence framework (COM-B, Health Belief Model) informs your outcome definition and feature selection? A clear conceptual framework prevents building a technically sound tool that misses the clinical reality.
D.Problem Statement▼
A precise, measurable statement of the clinical problem. Include: the population affected, the burden of the problem (epidemiological data), why current solutions are inadequate, and the specific gap this AI tool addresses. Avoid vague framing ("AI could improve care") in favour of specific claims ("current risk stratification tools for CKD progression in Malaysian primary care have AUC ≤0.72 and were not validated in Asian populations"). The more precisely defined the problem, the more evaluable the solution.
E.Objectives / Research Questions▼
State primary and secondary objectives using the PICO framework. Primary objective: what is the main thing the AI tool must achieve? Secondary objectives: subgroup analyses, fairness evaluations, usability assessments, health economic analysis. Frame as SMART objectives (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound). Each objective should have a corresponding outcome measure and statistical analysis plan. Reviewers should be able to assess whether the objectives were met from the results alone.
F.Methods▼
F1 — Study Design: Specify the design (prediction model development and validation, diagnostic accuracy study, clinical trial, real-world evidence study) and the corresponding reporting framework (TRIPOD+AI, CONSORT-AI, SPIRIT-AI, DECIDE-AI). Justify the design choice.

F2 — Population: Inclusion and exclusion criteria. Target population demographics. How the population was or will be identified. Expected sample characteristics relevant to model generalisability.

F3 — Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria: State explicitly. For AI studies this must include: data completeness thresholds, time windows for data extraction, handling of duplicate records, and criteria for excluding clinically atypical cases that might distort training.

F4 — Setting: Institution(s), care level (primary, secondary, tertiary), country and healthcare system context. For Malaysian studies: specify MOH vs university vs private setting. State any plans for multi-site validation.

F5 — Instruments and Tools: Data sources (EMR system, registry, wearable, imaging). AI platform or framework (TensorFlow, PyTorch, scikit-learn, cloud ML services). Explainability tools (SHAP, LIME, Grad-CAM). Evaluation instruments (clinical decision curve analysis tools, calibration plots). Version numbers matter for reproducibility.

F6 — Ethical Considerations: Ethics board name and approval reference number (or application status). PDPA compliance approach. Patient consent plan. Data de-identification method. Conflict of interest declaration. Data sharing and open science commitments. Regulatory classification (medical device or not) and registration plan if applicable. Bias monitoring protocol with subgroup performance analysis plan.
G.Sampling and Sample Size▼
For prediction model studies: justify sample size using the EPV (events per variable) rule — minimum 10 events per predictor variable for development, with a separate external validation cohort. For diagnostic accuracy studies: use published methods for sensitivity/specificity-based sample size (e.g. Obuchowski). For clinical trials: standard power calculations apply with AI as the intervention. State: total N needed, expected event rate, train/validation/test split rationale, and how class imbalance will be handled. Do not conflate model development sample size with clinical trial sample size — these serve different purposes.
H.Data Analysis Plan▼
Pre-specify all analyses before data extraction. Include: preprocessing pipeline (imputation strategy, normalisation, outlier handling, class imbalance correction). Feature selection approach and rationale. Algorithm(s) to be trained and compared. Internal validation approach (k-fold cross-validation). External validation approach. Primary performance metrics and their clinical justification (AUC, sensitivity/specificity at pre-specified threshold, calibration, net benefit). Subgroup performance analyses (sex, age group, ethnicity, comorbidity burden). Fairness metrics. Software and version numbers. Pre-registration of the analysis plan (e.g. OSF, ClinicalTrials.gov) is strongly recommended.
I.Discussion▼
In the proposal, the Discussion section is written prospectively: what do you anticipate finding, and why does it matter? Address the expected clinical significance of the results, how findings will be compared to existing tools and literature, and implementation feasibility — if the tool performs as hoped, what would be required to deploy it in practice? This demonstrates to reviewers that you have thought beyond model performance to real-world impact.

A well-written Discussion also shows anticipatory thoughtfulness about two distinct categories: shortfalls and limitations. These are not the same thing and must not be conflated.

Shortfalls are potential threats and biases that could undermine the integrity of the study — for example, selection bias in training data, label noise from inconsistent clinical annotation, temporal drift between training and deployment, or algorithmic underperformance in subgroups not well-represented in the dataset. In the proposal, shortfalls are raised prospectively alongside concrete mitigation strategies: how will you detect them, measure them, and reduce their impact? Naming a shortfall without a credible mitigation plan weakens the proposal. Naming it with one signals scientific rigour.

Limitations are the inherent imperfections of a valid, correctly designed study. They arise not from poor choices, but from the honest boundaries of what a rigorous study design can deliver for a given research question and setting. A single-centre dataset is a limitation if multi-centre validation is the gold standard — not because single-centre data is wrong to use, but because it is categorically correct yet insufficient for the highest level of evidence. A retrospective design may be the most feasible approach, but it remains a limitation relative to a prospective trial. These are declared honestly because they define the boundaries of what can be claimed from the results.

What limitations are not: they are not excuses for methodological errors, not confessions of slackness or shortcuts taken under time pressure, and not explanations of resource constraints that should have been planned for. If the sample size is inadequate for the stated objective, that is not a limitation — it is a design flaw. If a validation cohort is absent because it was unfeasible to obtain, that must be addressed in the methods, not hidden in limitations. Honest declarations of true limitations are expected and respected; misuse of the limitations section to explain away avoidable weaknesses is immediately apparent to experienced reviewers.

In the proposal stage, both shortfalls and limitations are anticipated — you are signalling awareness, not reporting failure. At study completion, these same declarations carry evidential weight: they define the scope of valid inference, guide the interpretation of results, and identify the gaps that future work must address.
J.Conclusion▼
A concise statement (2–3 sentences) of what the project will achieve, why it matters, and what it will contribute to the field and to patient care. Avoid overpromising. A well-scoped conclusion that matches the study design is more credible than an ambitious conclusion that exceeds the study's capacity to deliver. Explicitly connect back to the problem statement: if successful, this project will address the gap identified in section D by achieving the outcomes defined in section E.
K.Acknowledgements▼
Acknowledge funding sources (grant name, number, funding body), institutional support, data custodians who facilitated access, patient and public involvement contributors, and any AI tools used in preparing the proposal. For AI tool acknowledgement, state the tool name and version, what it was used for (language editing, literature search assistance, draft structuring), and confirm that all content was critically reviewed and verified by the authors. Follow the journal's or funder's AI disclosure policy.
L.References▼
Use a consistent reference manager (Zotero, Mendeley, EndNote). For AI tool development proposals, prioritise: the relevant reporting guideline (TRIPOD+AI, CONSORT-AI etc.), seminal papers on the clinical problem, existing AI tools in the same domain (to contextualise your contribution), and methodological references for your statistical approach. Avoid over-reliance on grey literature or preprints for clinical claims. Verify all AI-generated citations — hallucinated references are common and a serious scientific integrity issue.
M.Budget▼
Itemise costs across all four ETEPAI stages. Typical line items: personnel (data scientist, research assistant, clinical annotators — include FTE and duration), data infrastructure (cloud compute, storage, EMR access fees), software licences, model development compute costs, external validation (if multi-site: travel, coordination), clinical trial costs if applicable, regulatory fees (ethics board, medical device registration), dissemination (publication, conference), and post-deployment monitoring. Budget justification must link each cost directly to a specific study activity. AI-assisted budget tools can generate initial estimates — always have these reviewed by an experienced researcher and your institution's grants office.
N.Gantt Chart▼
Map activities across all four ETEPAI stages with realistic timelines. Critical milestones to include: ethics approval obtained (before data extraction), data governance agreement signed, training dataset assembled and labelled, model development complete, internal validation complete, external validation complete (before deployment), sandboxing period complete, full deployment, first performance review, and prospective impact study launch. Build in buffer for ethics review (typically 2–4 months in Malaysia), data access negotiations, and unexpected model iterations. A Gantt chart that shows every activity completing on time with no dependencies is not credible — show the real constraints.
O.Supplementary Materials▼
Include as appendices: the completed ETEPAI checklist for your project (self-assessment across all 4 stages × 3 domains), the relevant reporting guideline checklist (TRIPOD+AI, CONSORT-AI, etc.) with your planned responses, letters of support from data custodians and collaborating institutions, CVs of key team members, preliminary data or pilot results if available, and the data dictionary for your primary dataset. For clinical trial protocols: include the full statistical analysis plan. Supplementary materials demonstrate to reviewers that the project has been rigorously planned and that the team has the capacity to execute it.
+Ethical Use of AI & Acknowledgement Statements▼
A dedicated section — increasingly required by funders and journals — disclosing exactly how AI tools were used in preparing this proposal. State: which AI tools (name, version, provider), what they were used for (e.g. "ChatGPT-4o was used for initial literature search assistance and language editing of sections B and I"), what verification was performed ("all AI-generated content was independently checked against primary sources by BHC"), and confirmation of sole author responsibility for accuracy and integrity. Do not use AI to generate fabricated citations, invent data, or produce content you cannot verify. The MMC 2025 guideline and most international research ethics frameworks require transparent AI disclosure.
+Do's and Don'ts of AI-Assisted Proposal Writing▼
Do: Use AI to structure your thinking, generate first drafts for language-heavy sections, assist literature search, check grammar, and format references. Use AI to generate initial budget estimates and Gantt drafts. Use AI to summarise long guidelines into key points. Always critically review and verify every output.

Don't: Use AI to generate citations without verifying they exist. Let AI define your clinical problem or research question — these require your clinical expertise. Use AI to produce your statistical analysis plan without a biostatistician's review. Submit AI-generated content without disclosing it. Use AI to claim that your methodology is sound when it hasn't been reviewed by a qualified methodologist. Mistake fluent writing for accurate content.
